# Session 03 — Low Wall, Jump & Scent Loss (Kocher Method)

## General Information

**Level:** Advanced

**Method:** Kevin Kocher (Positive Trailing)

**Estimated duration:** 35-45 minutes per team

**Main objective:** The dog follows a trail along the low wall (1m) of an uninhabited plot, and must **mark the exact point where the figurant jumped over the wall** and disappeared to the other side — causing scent loss on the dog's side.

---

## Skills Trained

1. **Trail tracking** alongside a barrier (low plot wall)
2. **Jump point marking**: The dog indicates WHERE the figurant crossed the obstacle
3. **Scent loss management**: The trail disappears abruptly — the dog must communicate this
4. **Handler reading**: The handler correctly interprets that the dog has marked a vertical direction change
5. **Environment discrimination**: Inhabited houses (resident scents) vs. empty plot (clean figurant scent)

---

## Scenario Description

The location is a **rectangular city block** divided into **3 plots**:

| Plot | Type | Boundary |
|---|---|---|
| **Plot 1** (left) | Inhabited — house + garden | House fence/wall |
| **Plot 2** (center) | **NOT inhabited** — empty land | **Low wall 1m** (can be jumped) |
| **Plot 3** (right) | Inhabited — house + garden | House fence/wall |

The dog and figurant start **10m from the corner of Plot 1** (first house). The figurant walks along the sidewalk, passes in front of Plot 1, follows the low wall of Plot 2 to the **middle zone**, where they **jump the wall** and hide inside the empty plot.

### Phase 1: Dog Works — Start and Plot 1 (2-3 minutes)

- Present scent article (5-15 seconds)
- Verbal cue: "Search"
- Dog detects the trail, advances 10m to the corner
- Turns right and passes in front of **Plot 1** (inhabited house)

```
WHILE PASSING PLOT 1:

    🐕 ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ → (close to the fence)
    │
    ├─ POSSIBLE DISTRACTIONS:
    │  ┌─ Scents from house residents
    │  ├─ Pet scents (dogs, cats)
    │  ├─ Food / garden smells
    │  └─ Sounds from the house
    │
    ├─ Handler does NOT pull the line
    ├─ If dog investigates → wait
    └─ Must return to trail on its own
```

### Phase 2: Transition to Plot 2 and Low Wall Tracking

- Dog notices the change from fence (house) to low wall (empty plot)
- Trail "cleans up" — less contamination from resident scents
- Follows close to the low wall

### Phase 3: Jump Point — Key Moment (3-5 minutes)

- Dog reaches the middle zone of the wall where the figurant jumped
- Trail CUTS OFF abruptly — the wall continues but the scent does NOT
- Dog shows confusion → vertical check → **marks**
- Handler acknowledges the mark

### Phase 4: Resolution and Reward (2 minutes)

- Handler takes dog to the other side of the wall:
  - Option A: Go around through the plot corner
  - Option B: Help the dog jump (1m is accessible for many dogs)
- Dog finds the figurant crouching inside the empty plot
- **Maximum celebration** — reward proportional to effort

## Expected Behavior Table

| Point | Expected Behavior | If Not |
|---|---|---|
| Start (10m) | Detects trail, advances to corner | Hesitates → motivate from the corner |
| Plot 1 (house fence) | Follows trail, possible distractions from resident scents | Lingers sniffing fence → wait, don't pull |

## Kocher Method Notes

- **The dog LOSES the trail**: This is intentional. The goal is for the dog to **communicate** where the scent disappeared, not solve it on its own.
- **Marking ≠ lost**: The handler must distinguish between a lost dog (erratic) and a marking dog (focused on one point). This reading is fundamental.
- **Never force the dog past the jump point**: If the dog marks correctly, the exercise is complete. Finding the figurant is the reward, not the exercise objective.
- **No corrections**: If the dog gets lost, bring it back to the last trail point and let it retry. Never lead it directly to the figurant.
- **The 1m wall is ideal**: Low enough for the dog to detect residual scent at the top, but high enough that the crouching figurant isn't visible from the other side.
- **Empty plot = clean scent**: Since Plot 2 is uninhabited, there's no contamination from resident scents. The figurant's trail is the dominant scent in that zone — advantage for the dog.
- **Inhabited plots as distractors**: Scents from the houses (Plots 1 and 3) act as natural distractors. The dog must maintain focus on the figurant's trail when passing in front.
- **The middle zone is deliberate**: The figurant jumps in the middle zone of the wall (not at a corner) so the dog has to travel part of the wall before finding the jump point. If jumping at the corner, it would be too obvious.
