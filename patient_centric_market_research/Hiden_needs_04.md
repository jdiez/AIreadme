# AI Agentic Playground Protocol: Neuropsychiatric Manifestations in Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

Let me develop a comprehensive protocol for SLE neuropsychiatric manifestations—one of the most complex and challenging areas in rheumatology, affecting 30-40% of lupus patients but often remaining undiagnosed or misattributed.

## Why SLE Neuropsychiatric (NPSLE) Manifestations Are Particularly Hidden

### Unique Complexity Factors

**Clinical Heterogeneity:**
- 19 distinct NPSLE syndromes recognized by ACR
- Symptoms range from subtle cognitive dysfunction to acute psychosis, seizures, stroke
- Can affect central or peripheral nervous system
- Presentations overlap with medication side effects, infections, metabolic disturbances

**Attribution Challenges:**
- Difficult to distinguish NPSLE from primary psychiatric disorders
- Stress of chronic illness causes legitimate psychiatric symptoms
- Corticosteroid treatment causes psychiatric side effects
- No definitive diagnostic biomarker

**Demographic Factors:**
- Predominantly affects women of childbearing age
- Psychiatric symptoms often dismissed as "hormonal" or "emotional"
- Disproportionately affects Black, Hispanic, and Asian women who face additional diagnostic bias
- Young patients' symptoms attributed to "stress" or "adjustment issues"

**Systemic Complexity:**
- Multi-organ involvement makes symptom attribution difficult
- Fatigue, pain, and inflammation affect cognition independently
- Medication regimens are complex with multiple potential CNS effects
- Disease activity fluctuates, making symptom patterns unclear

**Agent 3: "The Steroid Psychosis vs. NPSLE Differentiator"**

**Role**: Distinguish corticosteroid-induced psychiatric symptoms from primary NPSLE

**Training Focus:**
- Steroid dose-response relationships
- Timing of symptom onset relative to steroid initiation/dose changes
- Symptom characteristics (steroid psychosis vs. NPSLE)
- Response to steroid reduction vs. immunosuppression
- Patient history of steroid reactions
- Baseline psychiatric symptoms before steroids

**Differentiation Framework:**
```
Analyze steroid-symptom relationships:

1. Temporal Correlations:
   - Did symptoms start within days-weeks of steroid initiation?
   - Do symptoms worsen with dose increases?
   - Do symptoms improve with dose reduction?
   - Were symptoms present before steroids?
   - Do symptoms persist after steroid discontinuation?

2. Dose Relationships:
   - What steroid dose was patient on at symptom onset?
   - Is there a clear dose-response pattern?
   - Do symptoms occur at lower doses (suggests NPSLE)?
   - History of tolerance at higher doses previously?

3. Symptom Characteristics:
   - Steroid psychosis: Often manic symptoms, insomnia, agitation, euphoria
   - NPSLE: More varied (psychosis, depression, cognitive, seizures)
   - Mixed picture suggesting both?

4. Response to Interventions:
   - Response to steroid taper
   - Response to immunosuppression
   - Response to psychiatric medications
   - Paradoxical worsening with steroid reduction (suggests NPSLE flare)

5. Risk Factors:
   - Prior steroid-induced psychiatric symptoms
   - Personal/family psychiatric history
   - Lupus disease activity
   - Other NPSLE manifestations present

6. Clinical Dilemma:
   - Need steroids for lupus control but causing psychiatric symptoms
   - Can't taper steroids due to disease activity
   - Uncertainty about primary etiology
   - Treatment paralysis
```
